Driving Impact in the Energy Markets

Energy markets are in turmoil. Again.

The 2026 Iran war and the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz have disrupted roughly 20% of global oil supply. Brent crude spiked to nearly $120 per barrel in March. European gas benchmarks doubled. The IEA called it the greatest global energy security challenge in history.

This comes on top of a crisis we never really resolved. Russia's war against Ukraine already exposed how dependent Western democracies are on autocratic regimes sitting on fossil reserves. Now a second geopolitical shock within four years is hammering the same structural weakness: we import too much energy from places we cannot rely on.

Progress and Backlash

The good news is that some countries are moving. The UK established Great British Energy in 2025 — a publicly owned energy company backed with over £8 billion to invest in clean power, storage, and supply chains. GBE aims to deliver at least 15 GW of clean energy generation and storage by 2030. That's the kind of institutional commitment the energy transition needs.

The bad news is that not everyone is pulling in the same direction. Germany's new government under Chancellor Merz has been systematically weakening climate policy. The coalition has repealed the renewable heating mandate, questioned the EU Emissions Trading Scheme, and sent mixed signals on EV adoption and wind expansion. The Climate Action Tracker rates Germany's efforts as "insufficient." The country's 2030 emissions gap is widening, not shrinking. Europe's largest economy is drifting further from its own legally binding targets — at the worst possible time. This is bad policy for climate, economy and global democracy alike.

Why I Got Involved

A friend of mine builds wind parks. One day he told me about an idea he was working on: using electric vehicles as batteries for the grid. The concept is called Vehicle-to-Grid, or V2G. I was intrigued. I started using AI tools to build models that could support his work — and quickly realised how deep the opportunity runs.

Why V2G Makes Sense for Public Transport

Electric buses are a perfect fit for V2G. They run fixed routes on predictable schedules. And they sit idle for 16 hours a day or more. With bidirectional charging, those parked buses become distributed energy storage assets. They charge when electricity is cheap and plentiful. They feed power back into the grid during peak demand.

Early V2G programs are already proving the economics. A single electric school bus in Massachusetts discharged over 10 MWh to the grid across two summers and generated $23,500 in revenue. Fleet operators are seeing $1,000 to $5,000 per vehicle per year. The technology is maturing fast — V2G production is scaling onto major commercial platforms in 2026, and eight US states have enacted or proposed legislation to support it.

From ISPs to Energy: The Surprising Similarities

Before energy, I spent years in telecoms and fiber networks. The parallels are striking. Fiber networks and power grids both require massive upfront capital investment. Both monetise through long-term subscriptions and usage fees. Both face complex regulatory environments and need to manage capacity against variable demand. The playbook transfers more than you'd think. How you model demand, plan network expansion, and price services — these are structurally similar problems.

Forecasting in Energy Markets: Lessons from Tech

At Google Fiber, forecasting drove profitability. Knowing where to build, how much capacity to provision, and when demand would materialise — these were the questions that separated good investments from bad ones.

In energy, the stakes are even higher. Every mismatch between supply and demand costs money. Imbalance charges eat into margins. And with renewables making up a growing share of the mix, the variability of supply makes accurate forecasting not just useful but essential.
